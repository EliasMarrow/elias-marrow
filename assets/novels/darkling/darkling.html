Darkling

Book I: Everterra

* * *

Prologue

Everterra is a land shaped by possibility and ruin in equal measure.

Its vastness resists comprehension, reaching from restless seas and ancient forests to sun-scoured deserts and places deliberately erased from memory. Across its long history, civilizations have risen, fractured, and vanished, leaving behind ruins half-buried by time and stories worn thin by retelling.

This is an age not yet forgotten.

It is called the Age of Men.

Once, the peoples of Everterra lived without great war. Conflict existed, as it always does, but it was contained — border skirmishes, rival claims, ambitions checked by balance rather than conquest. The races of the land lived alongside one another in uneasy tolerance, bound by custom, distance, and necessity.

That balance ended with the rise of a human king.

His name was Canaan.

At first, his ascent drew little notice. He emerged quietly, consolidating control over the southeastern reaches of Everterra through favors, bargains, and the convenient removal of those who resisted him. Many believed his rivals died by chance or misfortune. No proof ever surfaced to suggest otherwise.

By the time his intentions became visible, it was already too late.

Canaan ruled from Mount Canaan, a fortified metropolis that became both symbol and engine of his authority. In public, he was measured, charismatic, generous in speech. Behind closed doors, he ruled with precision and cruelty, reshaping the land through forceful diplomacy, bribery, and fear.

Lords who pledged allegiance were rewarded with titles, protection, and status. Those who did not vanished. Armies followed, not always by loyalty, but by necessity. Rank was offered freely to any able-bodied man willing to fight beneath his banner. Wealth was promised. Order was enforced.

Within five years, the South-Eastern Domain fell.

Within thirty, the majority of human civilization stood beneath his rule.

Canaan did not immediately wage war on the elder races. He did not need to. His power spread through infrastructure rather than invasion — fortresses raised at strategic crossings, castles converted into labor facilities, cities transformed into supply chains feeding his machine.

Prisoners were not executed.

They were repurposed.

Among the many compounds erected during this expansion, one inspired fear beyond all others.

The Ninth Gate.

Built against the northern edge of the Dark Forest, pressed close to the impassable icebound mountains that marked the end of the known world, the Ninth Gate was both institution and warning. It housed political dissidents, criminals, and the inconvenient — those who could not be allowed to exist freely but were still useful.

Useful things were not wasted.

The Ninth Gate was never meant to be lived in.

It was meant to be endured.

* * *

Chapter 1 — The Fire

The Ninth Gate was never meant to be lived in.

Seraph knew that before the guards ever touched him.

"No."

The word was barely sound — more breath than defiance — but it was enough.

"Move."

The command came without heat or haste, spoken the way one spoke to doors or animals. Hands seized the back of Seraph's robe and wrenched him forward. He resisted instinctively, boots scraping against stone, fingers clawing for purchase as the open doorway yawned before him. The air beyond it felt wrong — colder, heavier — as if the fortress exhaled only inward.

"Please," he said, voice thin, fraying. "Anywhere else. I'll clean anywhere else."

The blow came from the side. A fist drove into his ribs, sharp and precise.

Pain flared — bright, familiar, survivable.

Seraph folded but did not let go. His fingers dug into the threshold, nails scraping stone raw, as if the doorway itself might offer mercy. He had learned, over years, which pains passed and which did not. Beatings passed. Interiors lingered.

"Inside," a guard growled. "You know the rules."

They peeled his hands loose one at a time.

The pain was manageable.

What waited beyond was not.

They threw him.

Seraph struck the stone on his hands and knees, the impact jarring his bones. Cold dampness soaked instantly into his palms and sleeves. Behind him, the guards laughed — not loudly, not cruelly, just mildly entertained, as men were when violence required no effort.

"Always this one," someone said. "Acts like the walls are going to eat him."

"Give it time," another replied. "They usually do."

The door slammed shut.

Silence followed.

Not empty silence. Not peace.

A pressure. A presence.

Seraph remained where he had fallen, forehead hovering just above the stone, breath shallow and uneven. His ribs burned where they'd struck him. His hands trembled — not from pain, but from knowing where he was. The Ninth Gate did not merely contain rooms. It collected them. It remembered.

He had been beaten before.

Beatings passed.

The interior of the Ninth Gate did not.

He pressed his hands flat against the stone and breathed. One breath. Then another. He did not pray aloud — not here, not where the walls listened — but he held the habit of it in his chest like a coal held against cold. The Father was not a voice. Not a warmth, exactly. More the way a man holds certainty he cannot prove and carries it anyway, because the alternative is a different kind of weight entirely.

The chamber around him was vast, its ceiling lost to shadow far above. Water dripped steadily from unseen cracks, each drop echoing too long, as if the room were counting time. The air was cold and wet, heavy with mold, rot, and something older — something that had soaked into the stone and refused to leave.

Then hands touched him.

Firm. Certain.

"Easy," a voice said close to his ear. Calm. Grounded. "I've got you."

Seraph didn't rise.

He was lifted.

Strong arms drew him upright before his legs could fail him, steadying him as the world stopped spinning.

"You're shaking," the voice said.

"I'm fine," Seraph murmured automatically.

"You always say that," the voice replied.

When Seraph opened his eyes, there were three of them in the room.

Hugo stood closest — a broad, solid presence carved from endurance. Though shorter than most dwarves, his build carried unmistakable density: thick shoulders, powerful arms, weight that did not waste itself. His red hair hung long and bound into a single braid, his beard worn the same way, threaded with early gray. His clothes were sackcloth and rags, but he stood like a wall, positioned subtly between Seraph and the dark. There was something in the jaw, the particular set of it — something that did not belong to dwarf stock alone — but you would only notice if you were looking for it.

Behind them stood Garr.

Silent. Still.

The orc's presence was different — less weight, more balance. His frame was lean but powerful, muscle drawn tight beneath scarred skin. His movements were economical, deliberate. Long black hair fell loose down his back, untouched by ornament. His eyes were calm, observant, unreadable. Dark eyes. The kind that held things without announcing them.

Seraph himself was pale even by Darkling standards — white skin stark against the gloom, black eyes reflecting torchlight like polished stone. Lavender horns curved smoothly from his brow, dulled by years of grime. A tear in his robe revealed the slow movement of a double tail, twitching with nerves he could not still.

Three figures.

Three breaths.

Together.

Seraph closed his eyes for a moment longer than necessary.

He had tried not to come this deep into the Gate. He always did. And the guards always made sure he paid for trying.

But he was not alone.

He never was.

The chamber they had been assigned was enormous, far larger than any task demanded of it. Once, it had likely hosted feasts or councils — stone laid to impress, to intimidate, to endure. Now it stood hollow and stripped, its purpose reduced to neglect. The walls wept constantly, thin streams of water tracing paths into slick pools along the floor. Debris lay scattered everywhere: broken furniture, shattered stone, rusted iron warped by age.

They began to clean.

Brooms scraped. Stone shifted. Old things were moved and burned. Work in the Ninth Gate was less about progress than obedience — proof that bodies could still be directed.

"We've been here for years, Seraph," Hugo said, watching Garr move through controlled martial strikes against splintered furniture set upright like training foes. "And you still complain as if it will change our circumstances."

"I don't complain," Seraph said quietly. "I hope."

"That's what I said," Hugo replied.

Garr approached a solid oak table left inexplicably intact. He placed his palm against it, drew a slow breath, and struck downward. The table split nearly in half. Garr centered himself again, hands pressed together, breath steady.

Both brothers were long accustomed to Garr's silence. He had taken a vow nearly ten years earlier. Not even relentless beatings from the guards had broken it.

The scars along his back and calves told that story without words.

The work continued.

Stone shifted. Ash was gathered. Broken furniture was dragged closer to the fire pit and reduced piece by piece. The rhythm of labor settled back over them, familiar enough that Seraph almost forgot the tightness in his chest.

Almost.

They found the first nest late in the day — tucked beneath a collapsed section of wall, the stone hollowed by years of gnawing. A dozen rats, maybe more, disturbed into fury by the work crew's approach. They were enormous. Each the size of a large dog, bodies swollen and corded with muscle, fur patchy and slick with filth. Their eyes burned with fever-bright madness.

The three of them had been assigned rat work before.

They knew what to do.

Hugo moved first, drawing the creatures toward him with noise and motion, absorbing their attention. Garr worked the edges — patient, precise, ending each rat cleanly before moving to the next. Seraph stayed back, light gathering at his palms, using it not to fight but to drive, herding the ones that broke formation back toward the other two.

It wasn't elegant.

It was practiced.

When it was over, seven rats lay dead on the stone floor. Two had escaped into the walls. That was the way of it. You never killed them all. The Gate had been trying longer than any of them had been alive and the rats were still here.

Hugo crouched and began to dress the carcasses with the matter-of-fact efficiency of a man who had done this enough times that it required no thought.

"Save the good cuts," Seraph said.

"I know," Hugo replied.

Garr was already building the fire low and narrow, coaxing the flame down until it barely breathed. Anything that burned too brightly invited attention, and attention was never given without cost. Meat turned slowly on a rack of salvaged iron.

There was a calculus to rat meat in the Ninth Gate. The guards received food — not good food, not enough food, but food that arrived on a schedule and didn't have to be caught. Prisoners received what they could find, trade, or take. Rat meat sat near the top of what was findable. Salted and dried it lasted. It kept you working. It kept you breathing.

It was, by most measures, worth something.

"Save some," Seraph said again, when the smell of cooking meat began to reach the corridor. "For the others."

Hugo looked at him. Not argument. Not quite agreement.

"There won't be much to save," he said.

"Whatever there is."

Hugo hesitated, then sighed. "You're impossible."

The others Seraph meant were the older prisoners in the adjacent cell block — those too weak or too broken for rat work, who survived on whatever was passed through bars. Most of the Gate did not think about them. Most of the Gate could not afford to. Thinking about them required energy that hunger had already claimed.

Seraph thought about them anyway.

That was Seraph.

They ate what they needed. Wrapped what remained in cloth stripped from the hem of an already ruined garment.

When darkness fully settled, they lay close — not for comfort, but for certainty.

Morning would come whether they survived it or not.

Three bodies.

One warmth.

Held.

* * *

Rat work was routine in the Ninth Gate.

The assignments came down from the upper levels without explanation or sympathy — a corridor designation, a work order number, a tool allocation that never quite matched what the job required. Crews were assembled from whatever prisoners were available and sent in with brooms, clubs, and whatever courage they could scrape together.

Nobody expected the infestation to end.

That wasn't the point.

The point was that the rats needed to be pushed back periodically so the institution could continue functioning, and prisoners were the most economical way to push them. The crews came back thinner than they left, usually. Sometimes they came back with injuries. Occasionally they didn't come back at all, and a new work order was issued without comment.

In the cell blocks, the stories about missing crews circulated the way all stories circulated in the Gate — quietly, incompletely, amended by whoever was telling them until the original event was barely recognizable.

An older man named Cobb, who had been inside longer than he could accurately count, told Seraph about the lower levels one evening while they shared a wall in the corridor outside the meal line.

"Before your time," Cobb said, without preamble, the way men spoke when they weren't sure they'd have another chance. "They used to send crews further down. Past the second delineation. There's whole sections the guards stopped going into."

"Why?" Seraph asked.

Cobb looked at him the way men looked when a question confirmed something they'd already suspected.

"Crews went in," he said. "Didn't come out."

He said it without drama. That was what made it land.

"They blamed the rats," he continued. "Made sense, mostly. Rats that size, enough of them — " He shrugged. "But some of the guards said different things when they thought no one was listening. Said it wasn't rats. Said whatever's down there is something the rats avoid."

Seraph filed it away the way you filed things you couldn't afford to think about too much.

A week later, Hugo heard a different version from a prisoner called Maret, who had worked maintenance before a broken arm ended that assignment. Maret said the lower levels predated the current administration. Said there were records in the upper offices — he'd seen the covers once, being moved — that were older than anyone serving had been alive. Said the Gate had been built on top of something.

Hugo did not repeat this to Seraph.

He thought about it, alone, in the way he thought about things he couldn't do anything about.

Garr heard nothing specific. But he had spent enough time in the corridors to notice that certain guard rotations never went past a particular point on the east passage, and that a sealed iron door near the lower stairwell had rust at its hinges that suggested it had not been opened in a generation. He noticed the way some guards glanced at that door when they passed it — not fear, exactly. The particular blankness of people trained not to look at something.

He said nothing.

He noted it.

That was Garr.

* * *

Chapter 2 — The Passage

Morning in the Ninth Gate did not arrive with light.

It arrived with boots.

Seraph woke to pain first — a sharp impact against his ribs that forced the air from his lungs and snapped his eyes open. Stone scraped his cheek as he rolled instinctively, arms curling inward in practiced defense.

"Up."

The word came flat, without anger or urgency, as one might speak to furniture that needed moving.

Another kick followed, careless this time, delivered out of boredom more than malice.

"On your feet. All of you."

Hugo was already moving, pushing himself upright with a grunt, positioning his body between Seraph and the guards without making it obvious. Garr rose more slowly, unfolding from the floor with controlled ease, face unreadable.

"Rat detail," one of the guards said with a yawn. "Lower east wing. Don't get lost."

A laugh followed. Chains rattled. Keys jingled.

The door opened.

They were herded through the inner passages of the Gate, deeper than before. This corridor was narrower, the stone rougher, the ceiling lower. Moisture clung to the walls in thick patches, and the air smelled stale — untouched, as if even the rot had settled into stillness.

There were six of them on the work detail. The other three were men Seraph knew by sight but not well — a human called Daven who kept his head down and moved efficiently, and two others who had arrived together and spoke to no one. All of them carried the same tools: a club, a length of chain, a torch. All of them understood the assignment.

The corridor wound downward.

"Feels older," Hugo muttered.

"Everything here is old," Seraph replied quietly. "Some things just haven't been looked at in a while."

Hugo glanced at him — not disapproval, but concern — then returned his attention forward.

They found the first infestation quickly. It spilled from a side passage — eight or nine rats, smaller than the ones upstairs, but faster. The six prisoners worked without coordination, each managing their own space, the kind of loose cooperation that forms between people who share a task but not an alliance.

Nobody spoke much.

Nobody needed to.

The work had its own language.

They moved the bodies, stripped the useful ones, wrapped the meat in cloth. Daven moved efficiently and without comment. The other two took more than their share and nobody said anything because saying something required energy and certainty and the calculation wasn't obvious enough to be worth it.

Not yet.

They worked deeper into the corridor.

The second infestation was larger — a nest built into a collapsed section of wall, the stone eaten through by years of gnawing. The rats here were older, more aggressive, and the fight was longer and messier. One of the two quiet men took a bite to the forearm that bled badly. He wrapped it himself without asking for help and kept working.

By the end, the collected meat was substantial.

More than Seraph had seen from a single work detail in months.

He could feel the shift before it happened — the way hunger changed the quality of attention in the room. The two quiet men had drawn slightly closer together. Daven had stepped back. The meat sat between everyone and nobody.

"We split it even," Seraph said. "Six ways."

The larger of the two quiet men looked at him.

"Five," the man said. "Your friend can't eat."

He meant Garr. The vow — the silence — was common knowledge in the block. It had become, for some, an assumption of vacancy.

"He eats," Seraph said. "Same as anyone."

"He eats when we say," the man said. "We did more work."

This was not quite true. But hunger had its own arithmetic.

Seraph kept his voice measured. He explained, carefully, that all six of them had worked the same detail, taken the same risks, carried the same tools into the same corridor. That the man with the bitten arm had equal claim regardless of his silence. That a fair split prevented the kind of conflict that ended work details permanently — and that the guards, he noted, had a particular way of resolving prisoner disputes that benefited no one in the room.

The man listened.

He was not unreasonable. That was the thing about the Gate that you had to hold onto — most people were not unreasonable. They were hungry. Hunger and unreason wore the same face in the dark.

"Four, then," the man said. "And your kind gets nothing."

This was the part that was not about the meat.

Seraph kept talking.

Hugo said nothing.

He had been standing slightly behind Seraph, arms at his sides, the particular stillness of a man managing something internal. He absorbed the comment about Garr the way he absorbed most things directed at his brothers — with the quality of patience that was not quite patience, that was closer to a door held shut by force of will rather than ease.

He absorbed the second comment the same way.

The third — the one about Seraph specifically, about what the Darkling were worth, about the particular uses prisoners like him had in the lower blocks — that one Hugo did not absorb.

He moved forward.

Not a long movement. Not a dramatic one.

Just the deliberate closing of space that said certain things were finished.

The larger man read it correctly and stepped back.

His companion held still.

For a moment nothing happened.

Then the companion reached for the meat.

What followed was not a long fight. It was not a glorious one. It was three men — Hugo, then Garr when the second man entered it, then the quiet competent efficiency of people who had survived this long by being exactly what was required and nothing more — and it ended with two men on the ground and nobody dead.

Daven watched from the wall. He did not intervene in either direction.

When it was over, Hugo stood breathing hard, hands open at his sides. He was not angry. That was the thing that distinguished it from rage — there was no heat in his face, no wildness. Just the aftermath of necessity. Just the particular quiet of a man who had seen the line approach and stepped over it because there was no other direction.

The two men on the floor were not badly hurt. Hurt enough.

Garr gathered the meat without comment.

Seraph said nothing for a long moment.

Then: "Thank you."

Hugo did not reply.

He didn't need to.

They left the two men where they were and moved deeper into the corridor to finish the work detail. Nobody spoke about what had happened. There was nothing to say that the silence didn't already contain.

* * *

The corridor had a map.

Or it was supposed to.

The map was a smeared charcoal rendering on a square of cured leather, passed down through work details long enough that its original markings were barely legible beneath the amendments of everyone who had corrected it since. It showed the primary corridors, the secondary junctions, the stairwells.

It did not show what was past the third junction.

Someone had written there, in different ink, at a different time: *past here nothing confirmed.*

Seraph studied this notation for a moment when they stopped to rest.

"What does that mean?" he asked.

Daven, who had remained with them after the others left, glanced at it. "Means nobody came back to update the map," he said.

They moved on.

The third junction did not look different from the others. The corridor continued on the other side looking like every corridor — same stone, same moisture, same quality of dark. The torch bent slightly toward a draft that had no obvious source.

Seraph noted it.

They took the left turn because the map suggested left, and because the smell of rat was stronger in that direction, and because the assignment was the assignment regardless of what the map didn't say.

The fourth junction had no marking at all.

Hugo stopped. Held the torch up.

The corridor ahead was older. Not slightly older — the distinction was visible in the stonework, the way the masonry shifted from the measured precision of intentional construction to something rougher, less uniform. The walls here had been shaped rather than built. The ceiling was lower.

"We've come too far," Daven said quietly.

"We took the lefts," Seraph said. "The map said left."

"The map stopped saying anything three turns back."

They stood in the silence of that fact for a moment.

Hugo turned around.

The corridor behind them looked the same as the corridor ahead.

"Which way did we come from?" he said.

Nobody answered immediately.

* * *

They were not lost in the way of people who had made one wrong turn.

They were lost in the way of people who had made a series of small reasonable decisions inside a system that did not reward reason — a system older than anyone managing it, full of modifications and collapses and sealed sections and rerouted passages, a thing that had grown over generations until no single person understood the whole of it.

The Gate had not been designed to be knowable.

It had simply been added to.

They tried to retrace their steps and found that the corridor they had come through did not match their memory of it — not dramatically, not in any way that could be pointed to, just the particular wrongness of a space that had been modified enough times that it remembered several different versions of itself.

Daven sat down against a wall.

"I've heard stories," he said. It was not a complete sentence. He didn't finish it.

"About the lower sections," Seraph said.

Daven looked at him. "Old Cobb told you."

"Cobb told me something."

Daven was quiet for a moment. "The guards sealed this level. Not recently — years ago. Before my time inside. Something happened with a work crew and they sealed it and filed it as a collapsed section." He paused. "The crew was on the books as a transfer. That's what they do when they don't want to explain something. Transfer. Reassignment."

The Gate's language. Everything had a proper word that meant something else.

"You knew," Hugo said. It was not an accusation.

"I suspected," Daven said. "I didn't know. There's a difference."

There was.

They rested briefly. Shared what water they had. Then they moved, because resting in an unknown section of the Ninth Gate with a torch that would not last indefinitely was a form of dying by choice rather than circumstance.

* * *

The forgotten sections were not dramatic.

That was the first thing Seraph noticed when they came to them — there was no threshold, no moment of crossing. The corridor simply became something different without announcing itself. The stonework grew older and then older still, and then there were things in the dark that did not belong to any active part of the institution.

A row of rusted shackles bolted to a wall, the bolt housing half-collapsed into stone. Whatever the shackles had held was long gone — the iron itself was eaten thin by time.

A work station — a table, essentially — with tools still laid out on its surface, corroded past use, arranged with the care of someone who had expected to return.

A corridor branching left that ended after twenty paces in a collapse. On the floor before the rubble, a boot. One boot. The leather had hardened to something like wood.

"Someone left in a hurry," Hugo said.

Or didn't leave, Seraph thought. He did not say it.

The webbing appeared gradually, the way the older stonework had — first a thread here and there in the corners, then more, then enough that they were moving through it rather than past it, tearing it aside with their hands. It was old web — dried and brittle in places, but layered over with newer threads that had the quality of something actively maintained.

Garr stopped.

He crouched near the wall. Pressed two fingers into a thick cord of web and felt its tension.

He looked back at Hugo.

Hugo looked at the web. At the corridor ahead. At the torch.

"The stories were true," Seraph said quietly.

Not wonder. Not fear, exactly. The particular quality of a thing you had filed away as probably-not-real revealing itself as real, and all the space between those two states collapsing at once.

"We need to move," Hugo said.

They moved.

* * *

Chapter 3 — The Cavern

The passage did not open into freedom.

The tunnel they had found — not discovered, not solved, simply stumbled into through the accumulated wrong turns of a forgotten section of the Gate — twisted downward for a long time before it leveled. The stone changed again. Here it was not constructed at all. Just earth and rock, shaped by water over centuries, the kind of passage that predated any human intention.

The sound of water grew louder until it became constant.

When the tunnel finally opened, it did so suddenly.

The cavern beyond was vast.

Stone rose upward into shadow, jagged and ancient. A river cut through the cavern floor, black water moving steadily over smooth rock. Stalactites hung like teeth above them, dripping steadily into the flow.

Daven stopped at the entrance and did not come further.

"I'll go back," he said.

Hugo looked at him. "You'll get lost."

"I'm already lost," Daven said. "I know the Gate. I don't know what's out there." He said it without judgment. Just the plain statement of a man who understood his own limits. "I'll find my way back. File something. I don't know."

They looked at each other.

"Go," Seraph said. "Be careful."

Daven nodded once and disappeared back into the passage.

They followed the river.

It felt safer than choosing blind paths.

Time blurred. Muscles ached. Seraph lagged behind, breath growing shallow.

At the fork, Hugo stopped.

The river split in two directions — one narrow and fast-moving, the other wider, slower, vanishing into darkness.

Seraph sank down onto a rock before anyone could argue.

"I need a moment," he said. "Please."

Hugo opened his mouth, then closed it. Garr watched the tunnel behind them.

Seraph clasped his hands and bowed his head.

"I'll ask the Father," he said quietly. "For guidance."

Hugo turned his back, keeping watch. It was not dismissal. It was the particular silence of a man who did not share a belief but had stopped arguing with it — who had learned, over years, that Seraph's faith was not weakness dressed in gentle language. It had kept him standing in places that had broken other men. Hugo did not understand it. But he had stopped calling it nothing.

Garr moved a few paces away, eyes scanning the cavern.

Seraph closed his eyes and tried to make his breathing still enough for prayer.

The cavern was silent except for water.

Then he felt it.

A pressure.

A sense of closeness.

He opened his eyes and looked around.

Nothing.

He exhaled and closed them again.

He had just begun again when something touched his shoulder.

Then another.

Then another.

At first, he thought it was Hugo.

Or Garr.

Then a fourth hand closed around his arm.

Then a fifth.

Seraph froze.

This was not his brothers.

It happened too fast to understand.

Seraph opened his eyes.

Hands were on him.

Not Hugo's.

Not Garr's.

Too many.

A sharp pain bloomed between his shoulder blades — sudden, precise — and the world tilted violently. His vision dimmed, sound pulling away as if swallowed by distance. The cavern fell from beneath him, and he felt himself sinking, tumbling into a depth without bottom or form.

Then there was nothing.

Hugo sat at the river's edge, wiping water from his beard after quenching his thirst. The sound of flowing water filled the cavern, steady and indifferent.

Behind him, Garr knelt, splashing water over his face, methodical as always. When he rose and turned —

He froze.

Seraph was gone.

No.

There.

Wrapped in silk.

A massive spider clung to the cavern wall above them, its body grotesque and swollen, eight legs anchored effortlessly to stone. Thick strands of webbing cocooned Seraph midair, his limbs pinned tight against his body. His skin, already pale by nature, had taken on a ghostly hue — bloodless, empty. His eyes were half-lidded, unfocused.

Lifeless.

This was what the stories had meant.

This was what the sealed doors had been about. What the missing work crews had become. What the guards had filed as transfer and reassignment and collapse — all of it distilled into this single creature that had been here, in this cavern beneath the Gate, patient as stone, for longer than anyone currently alive had been keeping records.

Garr did not speak.

He did not hesitate.

He moved.

His body launched forward at full speed, passing Hugo in a blur. The suddenness of the motion snapped Hugo's attention toward the wall just in time to see Garr leap, arms outstretched, body parallel to the ground.

The spider reacted too late.

Garr collided with the cocoon, ripping Seraph free in a single motion and landing hard several feet away. The web tore with a wet snap as the spider screeched in fury.

Hugo was already moving.

He seized a makeshift staff — a broom handle reinforced with iron — and sprinted toward his brothers as the spider lunged to reclaim its prey.

"Garr!"

Hugo hurled the staff mid-stride.

Garr caught it effortlessly, spinning it into a ready stance as he centered himself. The spider abandoned Seraph and turned its attention to the two new threats, crouching low, legs tensed.

Garr struck first.

He leapt high, cresting into the air, and brought the staff down with brutal precision toward the spider's head. The blow landed solidly —

And barely slowed it.

The spider lashed out, its front legs snapping toward Garr. He rolled aside just in time, stone scraping his back as claws passed where his neck had been.

Hugo roared and charged.

He swung with both hands, slamming his full weight into one of the spider's side legs. The impact cracked against chitin with a sickening crunch. The spider screeched, retracting the damaged limb.

Seven to go, Hugo thought grimly.

The spider reared, abdomen lifting unnaturally high. Its spinnerets flexed.

Webbing shot forward.

It struck Hugo's feet, pinning his left leg to the stone in an instant. He staggered but stayed upright, still within striking range.

Garr reacted.

He slammed the base of the staff into the ground, using it like a pole. With a powerful vault, he launched himself upward, catching hold of the spider's sensory hairs near its head. He clung to it, riding the beast as it thrashed.

The spider twisted violently.

Another stream of webbing lashed out, wrapping around Hugo's other leg. He was pinned completely now — immobile.

The weapons they had were not enough.

Hugo knew it.

Garr knew it.

The spider began its six-legged stalk toward Hugo, mandibles clicking in anticipation.

Garr stomped down hard, driving his weight into the spider's head while wrenching backward on the sensory hairs. The spider reared up, shrieking — unprepared for the pain. Its balance faltered.

Hugo strained against the webbing with everything he had.

The bindings tore partially free, but a thick strand still tethered him to the stone behind.

The spider lunged.

Hugo rolled desperately, the web stretching taut as he barely avoided the mandibles. Garr yanked hard to the side, throwing the creature's trajectory off just enough.

Hugo scrambled to his feet.

An idea sparked.

He locked eyes with Garr.

No words passed between them.

The spider crouched, ready to leap again.

This time, Hugo didn't move.

The spider launched.

At the apex of its jump, Garr stomped and pulled with all his strength. The spider's head jerked upward in reflex, exposing the massive underside of its abdomen.

Hugo acted.

He struck the top of the staff with a single, brutal blow, snapping it into a jagged spear. He ducked low as the spider descended.

For a split second, there was darkness.

Weight.

Nothingness.

This is it, Hugo thought. The abyss. Seraph was right. I've doomed myself.

Then sensation returned.

The smell of damp stone.

The feel of dirt beneath his hands.

The spider convulsed above him, impaled, its body collapsing inward as viscous ichor spilled onto the cavern floor.

A hand seized Hugo's forearm and hauled him free.

He emerged gasping into the cavern light.

The spider writhed briefly, then fell still.

Hugo didn't look back.

He ran.

"Brother!" he shouted.

Seraph lay where Garr had placed him, unmoving. His eyes stared into nothing.

Hugo dropped beside him, pulling Seraph's head into his lap. He cradled him like a child, hands trembling.

"No. No, no, no — "

Moments earlier, Hugo had thought himself dead.

Now he wished he were.

Every argument. Every doubt. Every time he'd dismissed Seraph's faith flooded his mind at once. The Father. The protector. The hope.

Too late.

Seraph was gone.

Hugo's shoulders shook as he wept, grief breaking through him without restraint.

Garr stood nearby, silent, frozen.

He had never seen Hugo cry.

He had never seen Seraph so still.

Seraph had always been the one who healed. The one who brought life back from the edge. The one who believed when belief made no practical sense and turned out to be right often enough that you couldn't call it foolishness.

He was not the strongest.

Not the fastest.

But he was the thread that bound them.

Garr's thoughts moved without his permission — back to the first place he remembered being still, to shared hunger and shared warmth, to a time before chains and stone.

It was an orphanage.

He was crouched in the dirt, stacking sticks into what he imagined was a grand castle. The structure rose slowly, uneven and fragile, until it toppled after only a few layers. He tried again. And again.

Each time it fell.

Frustration welled in his chest, hot and immediate. He hung his head, shoulders sagging under the weight of a failure too large for a child to name.

A small hand touched his back.

"It's okay," a voice said.

Garr looked up.

The child standing before him was pale — skin and eyes alike, white as stone bleached by sun. A Darkling child. Despite the lack of color, there was something alive in him, something warm and bright. Though his eyes lacked pupils, they carried an unmistakable presence.

Seraph.

"I'm stupid," Garr said. His voice was small. "That's what the mistress says. I'll never be a builder. She says my kind is only good for strength and war."

This was before his oath of silence.

Seraph stepped around him, knelt, and gathered the fallen sticks.

"You just need support," Seraph said gently. He pressed the thicker sticks into the dirt, angling them into wide V-shapes at each corner. "Like this. Now they can hold weight."

He looked up and smiled. "You try."

Garr followed his instruction. Four sticks. Then more. The structure stood.

It didn't fall.

Then a second memory arrived — quieter, older, already half-formed before he could stop it.

He had been walking alone in the forest beyond the village, years deep into his vow of silence, seeking refuge from everything that required a response. A sound had caught him — panicked chirping, high in the branches. A cardinal. Trapped in a spider's web, a hunting spider nearby, its venom already in the bird's body. The bird had gone still. Eyes blank. Wings slack.

He had freed it with a stick. Held it. Begun to dig a small grave in the roots.

Then felt the breath against his palm.

Faint. Almost nothing.

The venom had not killed it.

It had only stilled it.

Life had waited.

Garr came back to himself with a gasp.

Hugo was still kneeling, tears falling onto Seraph's face.

Garr moved quickly. He knelt beside them and placed two fingers beneath Seraph's nose.

Warmth.

Breath.

Life.

He grabbed Hugo's shoulder and shook him hard.

Hugo looked up — rage and grief colliding — until he saw Garr's face.

The smile.

The tears.

"He's alive," Hugo whispered.

Relief broke through him like a flood.

They worked quickly. Hugo tore away the webbing and lifted Seraph with ease, slinging him over his shoulder.

The question remained — which way?

They explored the cavern's edges while Seraph lay still, Hugo refusing to let him out of arm's reach. The river had carved this place over centuries and it had carved more than one way through it. On the far side of the cavern, behind a shelf of rock that required climbing to reach, the water had eaten an opening into the stone — a passage, low and narrow, but passable.

It smelled of cold air.

Outside air.

Not the recycled damp of the Gate's lower levels but something that had traveled from somewhere open.

Hugo stood at the opening for a long moment.

"That way," he said.

He stepped forward.

Garr grabbed three river stones, heavy enough to break bone. Hugo adjusted Seraph's weight on his shoulder.

They walked.

The silence was heavy — not fearful, but resolute.

It had a voice of its own.

Keep going.

You must.

This is how you save him.

* * *

Chapter 4 — The Darkwood

The forest did not welcome them.

It absorbed them.

One moment there was the cave mouth, pale stone and the last gray trace of underground light. The next, trees. Old ones. The kind that had grown without permission for so long that the land beneath them had forgotten what sunlight felt like. Their roots broke the earth in great arching ridges, dark with moisture, slick underfoot. The canopy closed overhead like a held breath.

Hugo did not slow.

He carried Seraph across his shoulders the way he carried everything — without complaint, without performance, without acknowledging that his legs were already spent from the cavern and his ribs ached where the webbing had crushed them. He walked because stopping was not a choice he was willing to make.

Garr moved ahead, reading the ground.

They did not speak of direction. There was no direction, only forward, only away from the Gate, only deeper into a place that every man in every tavern in every city of Everterra agreed you did not enter and could not cross.

The Darkwood.

The stories were not exaggerations.

The insects came first — small ones, clouds of them, rising from standing water and rotting bark. Then larger. Things that did not exist elsewhere in Everterra, or if they did, had grown here into something worse. The air was wet and tasted like old earth. Sound traveled strangely. A branch cracking twenty feet away arrived a half second late, as if the forest processed noise on its own terms.

By the first night, they had eaten the last of what they carried.

By the second morning, Hugo's steps had shortened.

He did not say anything.

He did not need to.

Garr watched him and understood and said nothing in return. He stripped bark from a tree he recognized and gave Hugo the inner layer to chew. It would not fill him. It would give his jaw something to do, which was sometimes enough.

Seraph had not woken.

His color had not improved. The spider's venom sat in him like weather — not killing, but not releasing. His breathing was shallow and even and told them nothing. Hugo checked it so often that Garr had stopped counting.

On the third day, Hugo found a hollow between two roots and lowered Seraph into it with the care of a man handling something irreplaceable. He arranged him gently, checked his breathing, did not speak.

Then he sat beside him and did not move.

Garr watched him for a long moment.

Hugo's hands were flat on his knees. His eyes were open but not looking at anything.

Garr touched his shoulder once, briefly. Hugo did not respond.

Garr left them and moved into the trees.

* * *

He did not go far.

Far was not a luxury the Darkwood extended freely. The undergrowth thickened within thirty paces, roots rising to knee height, the ground between them pooled with black water that had no visible depth. He moved slowly, reading what he could. Some of the plants he recognized. Most he did not. He crouched beside a cluster of low leaves and pressed them between his fingers and brought them to his nose.

Wrong.

He moved on.

The light filtered down in pale columns, directionless. He had no sense of morning or afternoon. Only the quality of the silence, which had changed slightly — less insect noise, which meant either a predator nearby or weather shifting.

He paused.

Listened.

Then he saw it.

It sat on a branch at eye level, not three paces away.

A cardinal.

Impossible here. Wrong latitude, wrong forest, wrong everything. Birds like this did not live in the Darkwood. There was nothing in the Darkwood for them.

And yet.

It was very still. Its head tilted once, regarding him with one eye. The red was extraordinary — not the dull rust of a sick or molting bird but something that looked almost self-generated, as though the color came from inside the creature rather than its feathers. Against the gray-green dark of the forest it had the quality of an ember in a cold room.

Garr did not move.

The cardinal did not move.

Then the world went away.

* * *

Not darkness. Not unconsciousness.

Something else.

The forest fell from beneath him the way a dream sometimes falls — not collapsed, but withdrawn, as if it had simply stepped aside. What remained was nothing. Not the nothing of a dark room or a closed eye but the nothing of absence itself, total and textureless, carrying no temperature and no sound.

He was standing in it.

The cardinal was still there.

It sat on nothing, in nothing, its red somehow still burning.

Then it changed.

Not gradually. Not with motion. One moment it was a bird. The next it was a woman.

She was not tall. She was not remarkable by any measure that Garr could have named. Her face was tired in the permanent way of someone who had been tired for a very long time and had made peace with it. Her hands were the hands of work — marked, certain, familiar with use. Her eyes were dark.

He knew those eyes.

He did not know from where.

He did not know from when.

He only knew that something in his chest recognized them the way a body recognizes warmth — before the mind arrives, before the word exists.

The feeling that came with her was not comfortable. It was not the warmth of reunion or the sweetness of relief. It was something older and less reasonable. Something that rose from a place he had never looked at directly and had not known was still full.

His throat tightened.

His eyes burned.

He had not cried in a very long time.

He opened his mouth.

She raised one finger.

Gently. Precisely. The way you stop something small and important from falling.

She did not smile. She did not explain. She stepped close and leaned upward and put her lips near his ear, and she said something — a word or words or something that was not quite language but carried the weight of it — and he felt it settle into him like a stone settling into still water. Down through everything. To the bottom.

Then she was gone.

The forest came back.

* * *

He was on his knees.

He did not remember going down.

The cardinal was gone. The branch where it had sat was bare and gray like every other branch. The insects had resumed their noise. The light had the quality of late afternoon.

He stayed on his knees for a moment longer.

Then he stood.

He did not try to examine what had happened. He had not lived this long by demanding explanations from things that did not offer them. He had learned, early, that some things were simply true in the way gravity was true — not because anyone had agreed to it but because that was the shape of the world.

She had been there.

He knew what he had felt.

He knew what she had told him without telling him.

He began to move.

* * *

His hands found what they needed.

He did not fully know why he took each plant — only that the knowledge arrived with his hands before it arrived with his mind. Long dark leaves from a plant that grew beside standing water, the kind that drew fever outward. A pale root, thin as a finger, that smelled faintly of cold earth. Bark from a particular tree — he had to search for the right one, moving quietly through the undergrowth until he recognized the pattern of the wood beneath the gray outer layer.

He gathered everything without hurrying.

The light was failing properly now, moving toward darkness with the ordinary seriousness of evening.

He returned to Hugo.

Hugo had not moved. Seraph's breathing was the same — shallow, measured, telling nothing.

Garr crouched and laid out what he had gathered on a flat section of root. Hugo watched him. Said nothing. His eyes moved from the plants to Garr's face and back.

Garr did not look up.

He worked.

He knew, without being able to say why he knew, that the spider's venom was not a killing thing. It was a stilling thing. It pressed the body down into stillness the way a heavy hand presses water down — the water was still there, still moving in its deepest places, simply held.

The leaves needed bruising. He pressed them between two stones, patient and deliberate, until the bruised edges darkened and the smell changed into something sharper, something that belonged to breath and blood rather than to earth.

The root he split and laid open.

The bark he crumbled into his palm and breathed on slowly, warming it.

He worked the mixture together with river water from the skin he carried, coaxing it into something that could be used.

Hugo watched the whole time.

When Garr was ready he looked up and met Hugo's eyes.

Hugo nodded once.

Garr turned to Seraph.

He worked the medicine in slowly, carefully, with the patience of someone who understood that the body needed to be offered things rather than forced. A little at a time. The bruised leaves held against the throat where the venom had settled. The root across the wrists, the pulse points. The bark and water to the lips, eased in with a finger, a breath's worth at a time.

Then they waited.

The forest moved around them with its own indifferent life. Something large passed to the north. Insects returned. Water moved somewhere below and out of sight.

Hugo sat with his back against the root, one hand resting near Seraph's hand. Not holding it. Just near it.

Time passed without announcing itself.

Then Seraph's breathing changed.

Barely. A deepening, almost imperceptible, like a tide shifting direction at its lowest point. His color moved — not toward normal but away from the gray that had settled in him. His fingers moved once, briefly, and were still.

Hugo let out a breath he had been holding for three days.

He did not speak.

Garr did not look at him.

There was nothing to say that the silence did not already contain.

They stayed where they were as the dark fully arrived, close together in the way of people who do not need to discuss it.

Three bodies.

One warmth.

The Darkwood moved around them, indifferent and immense.

Morning would come whether they survived it or not.

But they had survived tonight.

And for now, that was enough.
