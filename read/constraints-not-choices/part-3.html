Part Three — Constraints, Not Choices

Evidence Without Morality

Chapter Five: Testing the World We Live In

The argument so far has been structural and theoretical. Constraint stacking and probability explain how failure can emerge without moral cause. But theory requires testing. The remaining question is whether these dynamics describe the world as it actually exists.

To answer this, the analysis turns to simulation — not to predict individual outcomes, but to test whether equal effort produces converging results under realistic conditions.

The model is built to be fair to the opposing view. It assumes rational behavior throughout. Agents work when work is available. Bills are paid when possible. No one sabotages themselves. There is no laziness parameter. There is no moral sorting of any kind.

The environment, however, includes ordinary risk calibrated to U.S. conditions: job loss, medical expense, transportation failure, rent obligations. These events occur at realistic frequencies. Nothing is extreme. Everything is ordinary.

A population of 1,500 agents is run across five simulated years — 260 weekly steps — and the entire simulation is repeated 120 times to ensure results are not artifacts of a particular random sequence.

The results are consistent and striking.

When no one has a stability advantage, roughly 96 out of 100 agents experience housing failure over five years. When a small group (5%) begins with better buffers and lower shock exposure, the rate drops modestly to 93. When that group grows to 10%, it drops to 90.

Stability helps. But the magnitude is small relative to the baseline. Most outcomes are still decided by timing, shock size, and how much room existed when something went wrong.

The result is not collapse. It is churn. Over time, different individuals occupy failure states, but the overall distribution remains steady. The simulation does not invent this pattern. It reflects the mechanics described in the previous chapters.

There is no moral gradient explaining loss. Failure persists because the system requires continuous clearance of thresholds.

The most significant aspect of these results is what they do not show. There is no dramatic separation between the diligent and the negligent. There is no virtue gradient. What appears is a steady failure rate that persists regardless of how individual behavior is specified — because the failure is not produced by individual behavior. It is produced by the conditions.

Chapter Six: Why a 10% Failure Rate Is the Signal, Not the Reassurance

A common reaction to simulation results showing minority failure is relief. If only 10 or 20 percent fail, the system appears fundamentally fair. Most people make it. The few who do not must have done something differently.

This reaction misunderstands what the number represents.

In constraint-based systems, a persistent minority failure rate is not an error signal. It is a stability signal. Systems that eliminate failure entirely lose the disciplinary pressure that keeps behavior in line. Systems that allow failure to dominate lose the legitimacy that keeps people participating. A stable failure band — somewhere in the range of 10 to 20 percent — is evidence that the system is operating within its functional range.

The number matters more than the narrative attached to it. Ten percent failure is not evidence of insufficient effort by those 10 percent. It is evidence of managed exposure. The system is calibrated to produce that level of failure, and it does so reliably.

Over time, the identities of those who fail change. The proportion does not. This churn is essential to how the system maintains legitimacy: because failure is dispersed across populations and generations, it remains explainable through individual stories. Each case can be moralized. The aggregate pattern stays invisible.

If effort were the deciding factor, failure rates would decline as norms and work ethic improved. They do not. They stabilize.

The issue is not why some people fail. The issue is why the system requires some people to fail in order to continue.

Once this is understood, debates about motivation and personal responsibility lose much of their explanatory force. The system is not sorting people by effort. It is regulating pressure — maintaining a failure band sufficient to discipline behavior without provoking mass withdrawal.
