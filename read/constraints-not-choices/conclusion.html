Conclusion — Constraints, Not Choices

This Is Not a Moral Argument

This book has resisted a familiar temptation: to explain outcomes by assigning virtue or blame. That temptation is understandable. Moral stories are comforting. They allow us to believe the world is sorted by intention rather than exposure, by character rather than conditions.

What has been shown instead is quieter and more difficult to accept. In systems built on recurring constraints, outcomes are shaped primarily by structure. Effort determines participation. It does not determine destination.

This does not deny responsibility. It relocates it. Responsibility governs behavior within a system. Structure governs what that behavior can plausibly achieve. Holding both ideas at once is harder than choosing either, but it is more honest.

When constraints stack and margins thin, probability replaces merit as the dominant sorting mechanism. Failure becomes a steady state, not an aberration. Its persistence does not imply cruelty or conspiracy. It implies function: pressure disciplines behavior, belief preserves legitimacy, opposition manages harm without confronting its source.

Understanding this does not lead to despair. It leads to clarity. Once the structure is visible, debates can move from character to conditions — from asking what is wrong with people to asking what is wrong with the arrangement.

The simulation results are not a prediction of individuals. They are a description of mechanics. Run the same conditions repeatedly, hold effort constant, vary only the exposure — and the pattern that emerges is not moral. It is mechanical. Failure clusters where margins are thinnest. Recovery concentrates where buffers exist.

The political consequences follow from the structural logic. Systems that depend on constraint face a permanent legitimacy problem, managed through opposition and periodic relief. Those management tools work until they stop working — until belief erodes faster than pressure can be recalibrated. At that point, history moves.

The future is not decided by exhortation or blame. It is decided by how much constraint a system can impose before belief breaks — and whether it can widen margins without losing coherence.

That choice remains unresolved. It cannot be made clearly until the world is seen as it is.

This is not a moral argument. It is a structural one. The consequences of mistaking one for the other have already been made clear.

Appendix: The Simulation

Structural Constraint Dominance in U.S.-Calibrated Monte Carlo Analysis

Overview. The simulation described in Chapter Five is a reproducible agent-based Monte Carlo model calibrated to contemporary U.S. economic conditions. Its purpose is to test whether aggregate hardship outcomes are explained by individual effort and stability, or by structural constraints and stochastic variance.

Merit and effort are explicitly controlled: all agents follow identical, rational decision rules. No group is assigned behavioral deficiencies. A counterfactual subgroup is given permanent stability advantages — higher starting buffers and lower shock exposure — representing the maximum plausible individual advantage without any behavioral superiority.

Model Design. Time advances in weekly steps over five simulated years (260 weeks). Each week, housed agents receive income if employed, pay baseline consumption costs, experience stochastic shocks at calibrated probabilities, and attempt to pay rent. Arrears accumulate when rent cannot be paid; eviction occurs when arrears pass a sustained threshold. Employment evolves through hazard rates for job loss and rehire. The permanently stable subgroup faces identical rules and obligations — only their volatility parameters differ.

Key Parameters. Constrained agents begin with $0–$3,500 in savings, face a 3.5% weekly shock probability, a 2.5% weekly job-loss hazard, and a 6.0% weekly rehire probability. Permanently stable agents begin with $7,000–$13,000, face 0.8% shock probability and 0.5% job-loss hazard. All agents earn $720 weekly when employed, pay $470 weekly rent, and $160 weekly for food. Eviction is triggered at 8 consecutive missed rent payments.

Results. Three scenarios were run — 0%, 5%, and 10% permanently stable agents — each replicated 120 times:

  • At 0% stable: 96.15 evictions per 100 agents (±0.09, 95% CI)
  • At 5% stable: 93.18 per 100 (±0.12)
  • At 10% stable: 90.05 per 100 (±0.12)

Food insecurity declined from 3.00% to 2.80% of agent-weeks across scenarios. Median end cash among housed agents rose from $3,707 to $15,917.

Increasing the permanently stable share reduces hardship incidence. The magnitude, however, is modest relative to the baseline dispersion produced by constraints and variance. This is the characteristic signature of a constraint-dominated system: individual stability helps, but aggregate outcomes remain governed by structure rather than effort.

Limitations. The model does not represent housing supply curves, geographic segregation, credit markets, healthcare access, childcare costs, or jurisdictional policy variation. Eviction is modeled as a threshold process rather than a legal filing process. These simplifications are intentional: the controlled design is sufficient to demonstrate the core result under conservative assumptions. A more fully specified model would likely show stronger constraint dominance, not weaker.

The constraint stacking analysis referenced in Chapter Three — showing failure rates of approximately 30%, 51%, and 65% under one, two, and three stacked constraints respectively — is derived from a separate, simpler simulation isolating that specific dynamic without the buffering effects modeled in the U.S.-calibrated version.
