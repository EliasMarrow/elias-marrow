Darkling — Book I: Everterra

Chapter 4 — The Darkwood

The forest did not welcome them.

It absorbed them.

One moment there was the cave mouth, pale stone and the last gray trace of underground light, the smell of wet rock and distant water still clinging to their clothes. The next, trees. Old ones. The kind that had grown without permission for so long that the land beneath them had forgotten what sunlight felt like. Their roots broke the earth in great arching ridges, dark with moisture, slick underfoot, forcing each step into a small negotiation between balance and exhaustion. The canopy closed overhead like a held breath, and what little light reached the forest floor arrived already exhausted, gray and directionless, the color leached out of it by however many hundred feet of leaf and branch it had to fight through to get there.

Hugo did not slow.

He carried Seraph across his shoulders the way he carried everything — without complaint, without performance, without acknowledging that his legs were already spent from the cavern and his ribs ached where the webbing had crushed them, a deep bone-ache that announced itself with every step and that he simply declined to listen to. He walked because stopping was not a choice he was willing to make.

Garr moved ahead, reading the ground, his eyes finding the places where the earth was solid enough to bear weight and the places where it would swallow a boot to the ankle, guiding them around both with small economical gestures that Hugo had long since learned to follow without question.

They did not speak of direction. There was no direction, only forward, only away from the Gate, only deeper into a place that every man in every tavern in every city of Everterra agreed you did not enter and could not cross.

The Darkwood.

The stories were not exaggerations.

The insects came first — small ones, clouds of them, rising from standing water and rotting bark, finding every exposed inch of skin with a persistence that made Hugo's jaw ache from clenching against it. Then larger. Things that did not exist elsewhere in Everterra, or if they did, had grown here into something worse — beetles the size of a fist, their carapaces clicking faintly as they moved through the undergrowth, things with too many legs that vanished into the black water before either brother could get a clear look at them. The air was wet and tasted like old earth, thick enough to feel in the lungs, a taste that coated the back of the throat and never quite washed away. Sound traveled strangely. A branch cracking twenty feet away arrived a half second late, as if the forest processed noise on its own terms, deciding for itself what was worth letting through and when.

By the first night, they had eaten the last of what they carried — a handful of dried rat meat, eaten without ceremony, swallowed more than tasted.

By the second morning, Hugo's steps had shortened.

He did not say anything.

He did not need to.

Garr watched him and understood and said nothing in return. He stripped bark from a tree he recognized — pale beneath the rough gray outer layer, faintly bitter when he tested a sliver of it against his tongue — and gave Hugo the inner layer to chew. It would not fill him. It would give his jaw something to do, which was sometimes enough.

Seraph had not woken.

His color had not improved. The spider's venom sat in him like weather — not killing, but not releasing. His breathing was shallow and even and told them nothing. Hugo checked it so often that Garr had stopped counting, had stopped really seeing it as a separate action at all, just another part of the rhythm of walking — step, step, check, step, step, check, on and on through the gray directionless light.

The hunger settled into both of them differently. For Hugo it arrived as a hollowness that made his hands shake faintly when he set Seraph down each night, a tremor he hid by keeping his hands busy. For Garr it arrived as a thinning of everything — sound, color, the edges of his own thoughts — as though his body had begun quietly rationing not just food but attention itself, spending only what each moment strictly required and saving the rest for whatever came next.

On the third day, Hugo found a hollow between two roots and lowered Seraph into it with the care of a man handling something irreplaceable. He arranged him gently, checked his breathing, did not speak.

Then he sat beside him and did not move.

Garr watched him for a long moment.

Hugo's hands were flat on his knees. His eyes were open but not looking at anything.

Garr touched his shoulder once, briefly. Hugo did not respond.

Garr left them and moved into the trees.

He did not go far.

Far was not a luxury the Darkwood extended freely. The undergrowth thickened within thirty paces, roots rising to knee height, the ground between them pooled with black water that had no visible depth, the kind of water that gave no hint whether it was an inch deep or bottomless, that simply received whatever was dropped into it without comment. He moved slowly, reading what he could. Some of the plants he recognized — the shapes of things his mother, or someone's mother, or some half-remembered teacher in the orphanage's kitchen garden, had once shown him. Most he did not. He crouched beside a cluster of low leaves and pressed them between his fingers and brought them to his nose.

Wrong.

He moved on.

The light filtered down in pale columns, directionless. He had no sense of morning or afternoon — the Darkwood kept its own time, indifferent to the sun's actual position, doling out its gray light in quantities that bore no obvious relationship to the hour. Only the quality of the silence told him anything at all, and it had changed slightly — less insect noise, which meant either a predator nearby or weather shifting, the forest's two great organizing principles, threat and storm, the only events that seemed to matter enough to quiet it.

He paused.

Listened.

Then he saw it.

It sat on a branch at eye level, not three paces away.

A cardinal.

Impossible here. Wrong latitude, wrong forest, wrong everything. Birds like this did not live in the Darkwood. There was nothing in the Darkwood for them — no clean seed, no open sky, nothing a cardinal's small bright life was built to use.

And yet.

It was very still. Its head tilted once, regarding him with one eye. The red was extraordinary — not the dull rust of a sick or molting bird but something that looked almost self-generated, as though the color came from inside the creature rather than its feathers. Against the gray-green dark of the forest it had the quality of an ember in a cold room.

Garr did not move.

The cardinal did not move.

Then the world went away.

Not darkness. Not unconsciousness.

Something else.

The forest fell from beneath him the way a dream sometimes falls — not collapsed, but withdrawn, as if it had simply stepped aside. What remained was nothing. Not the nothing of a dark room or a closed eye but the nothing of absence itself, total and textureless, carrying no temperature and no sound.

He was standing in it.

The cardinal was still there.

It sat on nothing, in nothing, its red somehow still burning.

Then it changed.

Not gradually. Not with motion. One moment it was a bird. The next it was a woman.

She was not tall. She was not remarkable by any measure that Garr could have named. Her face was tired in the permanent way of someone who had been tired for a very long time and had made peace with it. Her hands were the hands of work — marked, certain, familiar with use. Her eyes were dark.

He knew those eyes.

He did not know from where.

He did not know from when.

He only knew that something in his chest recognized them the way a body recognizes warmth — before the mind arrives, before the word exists.

The feeling that came with her was not comfortable. It was not the warmth of reunion or the sweetness of relief. It was something older and less reasonable. Something that rose from a place he had never looked at directly and had not known was still full.

His throat tightened.

His eyes burned.

He had not cried in a very long time.

He opened his mouth.

She raised one finger.

Gently. Precisely. The way you stop something small and important from falling.

She did not smile. She did not explain. She stepped close and leaned upward and put her lips near his ear, and she said something — a word or words or something that was not quite language but carried the weight of it — and he felt it settle into him like a stone settling into still water. Down through everything. To the bottom.

Then she was gone.

The forest came back.

He was on his knees.

He did not remember going down.

The cardinal was gone. The branch where it had sat was bare and gray like every other branch. The insects had resumed their noise. The light had the quality of late afternoon.

He stayed on his knees for a moment longer.

Then he stood.

He did not try to examine what had happened. He had not lived this long by demanding explanations from things that did not offer them. He had learned, early, that some things were simply true in the way gravity was true — not because anyone had agreed to it but because that was the shape of the world.

She had been there.

He knew what he had felt.

He knew what she had told him without telling him.

He began to move.

His hands found what they needed.

He did not fully know why he took each plant — only that the knowledge arrived with his hands before it arrived with his mind. Long dark leaves from a plant that grew beside standing water, the kind that drew fever outward, their undersides faintly sticky to the touch. A pale root, thin as a finger, that smelled faintly of cold earth when he broke its skin. Bark from a particular tree — he had to search for the right one, moving quietly through the undergrowth until he recognized the pattern of the wood beneath the gray outer layer, a faint reddish grain that none of the surrounding trees shared.

He gathered everything without hurrying, his hands moving with a certainty that felt borrowed from somewhere outside himself.

The light was failing properly now, moving toward darkness with the ordinary seriousness of evening rather than the suspended murk of the deep forest afternoon.

He returned to Hugo.

Hugo had not moved. Seraph's breathing was the same — shallow, measured, telling nothing.

Garr crouched and laid out what he had gathered on a flat section of root. Hugo watched him. Said nothing. His eyes moved from the plants to Garr's face and back, something in his expression suggesting he understood that whatever had happened to Garr in the trees had not been nothing, even if he couldn't have said what it was.

Garr did not look up.

He worked.

He knew, without being able to say why he knew, that the spider's venom was not a killing thing. It was a stilling thing. It pressed the body down into stillness the way a heavy hand presses water down — the water was still there, still moving in its deepest places, simply held.

The leaves needed bruising. He pressed them between two stones, patient and deliberate, until the bruised edges darkened and the smell changed into something sharper, something that belonged to breath and blood rather than to earth.

The root he split and laid open, the pale flesh inside it weeping a thin clear sap.

The bark he crumbled into his palm and breathed on slowly, warming it, the small dust of it darkening faintly with the warmth of his breath.

He worked the mixture together with river water from the skin he carried, coaxing it into something that could be used, a thick dark paste that smelled of bitterness and earth and something underneath both that Garr couldn't name.

Hugo watched the whole time.

When Garr was ready he looked up and met Hugo's eyes.

Hugo nodded once.

Garr turned to Seraph.

He worked the medicine in slowly, carefully, with the patience of someone who understood that the body needed to be offered things rather than forced. A little at a time. The bruised leaves held against the throat where the venom had settled, the skin there cool to the touch in a way that worried him more than he let his face show. The root across the wrists, the pulse points. The bark and water to the lips, eased in with a finger, a breath's worth at a time.

Then they waited.

The forest moved around them with its own indifferent life. Something large passed to the north, heavy enough to make the ground tremble faintly, gone before either of them could decide whether to be afraid of it. Insects returned, settling back into their constant low hum. Water moved somewhere below and out of sight, patient, endless.

Hugo sat with his back against the root, one hand resting near Seraph's hand. Not holding it. Just near it.

Time passed without announcing itself.

Then Seraph's breathing changed.

Barely. A deepening, almost imperceptible, like a tide shifting direction at its lowest point. His color moved — not toward normal but away from the gray that had settled in him. His fingers moved once, briefly, and were still.

Hugo let out a breath he had been holding for three days.

He did not speak.

Garr did not look at him.

There was nothing to say that the silence did not already contain.

They stayed where they were as the dark fully arrived, close together in the way of people who do not need to discuss it, the forest's night sounds rising slowly around them — things calling and answering in the canopy far above, things moving through the black water that none of them would ever see clearly.

Three bodies.

One warmth.

The Darkwood moved around them, indifferent and immense.

Morning would come whether they survived it or not.

But they had survived tonight.

And for now, that was enough.
