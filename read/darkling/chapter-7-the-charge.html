Chapter 7 — The Charge

The charges were read in the square three days later.

Refusal to obey a lawful order.

Withholding evidence pertaining to crimes against the crown.

The official who read them was a man named Vance, sent down from the regional seat specifically for this purpose, a thin precise man who had spent eleven years reading documents like this one in towns he could no longer keep straight in his memory. He read them from a document in a flat, practiced voice — the voice of a man who had read documents like this before and understood that his job was to read them clearly and without editorializing and then return to wherever he had come from. He did not look at the crowd while he read. He had learned, early in his service, that looking at the crowd made the reading harder, and he had stopped doing it sometime during his third year, and had never started again.

The town stood in the square and listened.

They did not speak.

They did not need to.

They had been in this square three weeks earlier. They had watched Seraph sit at the table with the ledgers and try to explain the numbers. They had watched him go down under the guards' hands. They had watched it continue past the point of demonstration into the territory of something else.

They knew what refusal to obey meant when it appeared on a document like this.

They knew what withholding evidence meant when the evidence in question was the location of a man whose only crime was surviving something the crown had caused.

The language was careful. It was always careful. The system had spent generations learning to be careful with its language — to find the words that made what was being done sound like what it was supposed to be rather than what it was. Reform instead of punishment. Reassignment instead of disappearance. Refusal to obey instead of we need someone to blame and he is available.

The people of Idlewood were not scholars. They were farmers and laborers and tradespeople who had spent their lives learning to survive in a system that required their cooperation and rewarded their silence.

But they were not stupid.

And they had been in the square.

Vance finished reading and rolled the document with the same precise motion he used every time, tying it with the same length of cord he kept for exactly this purpose, and left without meeting anyone's eyes. He would be back at the regional seat by evening. He would file a copy of the charges, note the date and disposition, and move on to the next document on a desk that never seemed to empty.

The town stood there for a moment after he was gone.

Then, one by one, they went back to what they had been doing — not because the moment had passed, but because there was nothing else available to do with it, no sanctioned place to put it, and the wheat still needed bringing in before the frost deepened, and the animals still needed feeding, and a life, even a life carrying this particular weight now, still required tending.

Seraph was held for three days in a small stone building at the edge of town that had once served as a tool shed for the original mill and had been repurposed, over the years, into a holding cell whenever the crown required one, which was rarely enough that most of the town had forgotten it had a second purpose at all.

Corren posted two guards on it, rotating shifts. One of them was a young man named Joss, barely past his own training, who found himself, on his second night of watch, unable to keep from glancing at the small barred window more often than the assignment strictly required. He brought Seraph water without being asked to. He said almost nothing while he did it, and Seraph thanked him quietly each time, and neither of them spoke of what was coming, though both understood it equally well.

On the second night, Joss lingered at the window a moment longer than necessary.

"Why didn't you run?" he asked. "When you had the chance. Before any of this."

Seraph considered the question with the same seriousness he gave everything.

"Because someone would have come looking," he said. "And whoever they found instead of me would have paid for my absence."

Joss had no answer for that. He moved on to finish his rounds, and did not linger at the window again, though he found himself thinking about the answer for longer than he wanted to admit.

The other guard, an older man named Dett, felt no such complication. He had done this work for fifteen years and had learned not to ask the prisoners questions, because questions invited answers, and answers had a way of becoming difficult to forget. He did his rounds. He said nothing. He told himself, the way he had told himself many times before, that this was simply the work, and the work was not his to judge, only to perform.

He did not entirely believe himself. He had stopped expecting to.

The execution was on a Friday morning.

They brought Seraph into the square the way they brought everything into the square — procedurally, with the correct number of guards and the correct documentation and the correct official language. Everything had its proper form. The form was the point. The form said that what was happening was sanctioned, legitimate, the ordinary operation of authority rather than what it was.

The town came because not coming was its own kind of statement.

They stood in the square in the cold morning air, breath visible in small white clouds, and they watched. Pell stood with his wife, Edder held tightly between them. Calla stood near the well, her hands folded in front of her, knuckles white. Ossa stood at the edge of the crowd, his arms crossed, his jaw set in a way that made him look older than he was. Tobren stood near the chapel's edge of the square, a small unlit candle held in one cupped hand, as though he had brought it meaning to light it and had thought better of the gesture once he arrived. Finn and Brask stood together, the men who had helped dig in the orchard's soft soil three weeks before, neither of them able to look directly at the small wooden platform that had been erected in the square's center sometime in the night.

Seraph was brought through the crowd on foot. His hands were bound in front of him. He had been held for three days in the old tool shed and he was thinner for it, marked by it, but upright.

He walked without being pushed.

Corren walked beside him and kept a hand at his arm but the hand was not gripping, was not forcing — Seraph was walking and the hand was simply there, a formality, the gesture of custody rather than its actual exercise. Corren's face, in the gray morning light, held none of the satisfaction a man might expect from completing a difficult assignment. It held something closer to the particular emptiness of a soldier executing an order he had stopped, somewhere in the last three weeks, being entirely sure he believed in.

The crowd parted for him.

He looked at them as he passed.

Not at the crowd as a mass. At people. Individual faces. The woman who had brought food without announcement. The man who had helped clear the barn without being asked. The woman who had once pulled her children close in the lane and later left bread on his desk without a word. Edder on a fence post at the edge of the square, serious-faced, ten years old and carrying something in his eyes that ten-year-olds shouldn't have to carry.

Seraph looked at the boy.

The boy looked back at him.

Something passed between them that wasn't language and didn't need to be.

They brought Seraph to the center of the square, to the platform, and Vance — who had ridden back out from the regional seat specifically for this, because the reading of a charge and the carrying out of its sentence required the same official presence, a detail of procedure nobody in the chain above him had ever questioned — unrolled the document a second time.

The official read the charges again. Same flat voice. Same careful language. The words fell into the cold air and the crowd received them in silence and the silence had a texture to it — not the silence of acceptance, not the silence of fear exactly, but the silence of people holding something that they had no sanctioned way to put down.

Seraph stood through the reading.

His face was what his face was.

The morning was cold and gray and ordinary in the way that the worst mornings often were — indifferent to the significance of what was happening in them, unwilling to provide the weather that the moment seemed to demand. Somewhere beyond the square a rooster called, oblivious, and was answered by another, and the ordinary business of the world continued in the margins of the square exactly as it always had.

When the reading finished, Seraph turned slightly toward the crowd.

Corren's hand tightened at his arm.

Seraph did not pull away.

He just turned, slightly, and looked at the people of Idlewood.

He did not speak.

He did not raise his bound hands in any dramatic gesture.

He simply looked at them.

And then, very slightly, he nodded.

Not the nod of a man making a speech or delivering a message or trying to inspire. The nod of a man who saw the people in front of him and wanted them to know that he saw them. That was all. That he saw them and that it was alright and that whatever came next, this moment — this particular gathering of ordinary people in a cold square on a gray morning — had been real, and he had been part of it, and that mattered.

The crowd received it in silence.

Then it was over.
