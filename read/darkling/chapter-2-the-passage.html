Darkling — Book I: Everterra

Chapter 2 — The Passage

Morning in the Ninth Gate did not arrive with light.

It arrived with boots.

Seraph woke to pain first — a sharp impact against his ribs that forced the air from his lungs and snapped his eyes open. Stone scraped his cheek as he rolled instinctively, arms curling inward in practiced defense, the motion older than thought, the body's own archive of every morning that had come before this one.

"Up."

The word came flat, without anger or urgency, as one might speak to furniture that needed moving.

Another kick followed, careless this time, delivered out of boredom more than malice.

"On your feet. All of you."

Hugo was already moving, pushing himself upright with a grunt, positioning his body between Seraph and the guards without making it obvious — a small shift of weight, nothing that could be called defiance, nothing a guard could object to and still feel reasonable doing so. Garr rose more slowly, unfolding from the floor with controlled ease, face unreadable, the stiffness of yesterday's labor working itself loose in his shoulders.

"Rat detail," one of the guards said with a yawn, scratching at something beneath his collar. "Lower east wing. Don't get lost."

A laugh followed. Chains rattled. Keys jingled, the particular sound of a man who carried more of them than he needed to, the weight of authority made audible.

The door opened.

They were herded through the inner passages of the Gate, deeper than before, descending by a staircase that had been carved rather than built, the steps uneven in a way that suggested whoever cut them had not expected anyone of consequence to use them often. This corridor was narrower, the stone rougher, the ceiling low enough that Hugo had to duck in places, his shoulders nearly brushing both walls at once. Moisture clung to the walls in thick patches, and the air smelled stale — untouched, as if even the rot had settled into stillness, a smell with no movement in it at all, the smell of a sealed jar.

There were six of them on the work detail.

The other three were men Seraph knew by sight but not well. A human called Daven, lean and economical in his movements, who kept his head down and worked efficiently and rarely spoke unless spoken to — Seraph had shared a meal line with him perhaps a dozen times over the years and knew almost nothing about him beyond the fact that he'd arrived sometime before either Seraph or his brothers had, and that he had a way of watching a room that suggested he was always calculating something, quietly, behind a face that gave away nothing.

Then there were two others. Brothers, Seraph thought, though he'd never confirmed it — they had the same heavy brow, the same way of standing close to one another without seeming to notice they were doing it. They had arrived together some months back, transferred in from another section of the Gate following some infraction neither of them discussed, and they spoke to almost no one but each other, a low murmur of conversation that stopped whenever anyone came near enough to overhear it.

All of them carried the same tools: a club, a length of chain, a torch. All of them understood the assignment, in the way that understanding accumulated in the Gate — not through explanation, but through repetition, until a body knew what was required of it before the mind had finished hearing the order.

The corridor wound downward.

"Feels older," Hugo muttered, running a hand along the stone as they passed, feeling the change in texture beneath his palm — smoother in places, worn by water rather than tools.

"Everything here is old," Seraph replied quietly. "Some things just haven't been looked at in a while."

Hugo glanced at him — not disapproval, but concern — then returned his attention forward.

They found the first infestation quickly. It spilled from a side passage — eight or nine rats, smaller than the ones upstairs, but faster, moving with the quick low scrabble of creatures that had learned exactly how much warning a torch gave them. The six prisoners worked without coordination, each managing their own space, the kind of loose cooperation that forms between people who share a task but not an alliance — nobody calling out warnings to anyone but themselves, nobody covering a stranger's back unless the alternative was their own exposed throat.

Nobody spoke much.

Nobody needed to.

The work had its own language.

They moved the bodies, stripped the useful ones, wrapped the meat in cloth carried for exactly this purpose. Daven moved efficiently and without comment, his knife finding the cuts that mattered with the speed of long practice. The two brothers took more than their share and nobody said anything because saying something required energy and certainty and the calculation wasn't obvious enough to be worth it.

Not yet.

They worked deeper into the corridor, the torchlight throwing their shadows long and strange against walls that bowed inward in places, as if the stone itself had been pressed by some great weight from above and had simply learned to bear it without complaint.

The second infestation was larger — a nest built into a collapsed section of wall, the stone eaten through by years of gnawing into something that resembled a honeycomb more than a hole, dozens of small tunnels branching into darkness. The rats here were older, more aggressive, accustomed to defending territory rather than fleeing it, and the fight was longer and messier than the first. One of the brothers took a bite to the forearm that bled badly, dark in the torchlight, soaking quickly through his sleeve. He wrapped it himself without asking for help, tearing a strip from the hem of his shirt and binding it tight with his teeth and his free hand, and kept working, his jaw set hard against whatever he wasn't saying.

By the end, the collected meat was substantial.

More than Seraph had seen from a single work detail in months — enough that the cloth sacks they carried sagged with real weight, enough to feel, for a moment, like abundance, which was a feeling so unfamiliar in the Gate that it took Seraph a moment to recognize the unease underneath it.

He could feel the shift before it happened — the way hunger changed the quality of attention in the room, the way a held breath changes a room before anyone has spoken. The two brothers had drawn slightly closer together, a small instinctive consolidation, the body language of men preparing to defend something. Daven had stepped back, putting half a pace of distance between himself and what was coming, the posture of a man who had learned, somewhere, that proximity to a dispute was its own kind of liability. The meat sat between everyone and nobody, on a square of cloth laid across the broken stone, six pairs of eyes finding their way to it and away from it in turns.

"We split it even," Seraph said. "Six ways."

The larger of the two brothers looked at him. He had a face built for suspicion — heavy features, a scar through one eyebrow that pulled the skin slightly when he frowned, which was often.

"Five," the man said. "Your friend can't eat."

He meant Garr. The vow — the silence — was common knowledge in the block by now, repeated and distorted the way everything was repeated and distorted, until for some prisoners it had stopped being a fact about a man and become instead a kind of permission, an excuse to subtract him from any accounting that mattered.

"He eats," Seraph said. "Same as anyone."

"He eats when we say," the man said. "We did more work."

This was not quite true. The brothers had killed perhaps four rats between them; Hugo alone had killed six. But hunger had its own arithmetic, one that didn't always trouble itself with accuracy.

Seraph kept his voice measured, the same measured voice he used for everything, the voice that had talked down guards and calmed frightened cellmates and, once, convinced an overseer not to report a missed quota. He explained, carefully, that all six of them had worked the same detail, taken the same risks, carried the same tools into the same corridor. That the man with the bitten arm — and here he nodded toward the brother still holding his wrapped forearm against his chest — had equal claim regardless of anyone's silence. That a fair split prevented the kind of conflict that ended work details permanently, that brought guards down into corridors they otherwise left alone, guards who had their own particular way of resolving prisoner disputes that benefited no one currently standing in this corridor.

The man listened.

He was not unreasonable. That was the thing about the Gate that you had to hold onto, the thing Seraph reminded himself of more often than he let anyone know — most people here were not unreasonable. They were hungry, and had been hungry long enough that hunger had reshaped the architecture of what they were willing to consider fair. Hunger and unreason wore the same face in the dark, and it took real effort, real cost, to tell them apart.

"Four, then," the man said. "And your kind gets nothing."

This was the part that was not about the meat.

Seraph kept talking. He kept his hands open, his voice level, reaching for the words that might still bridge the distance — pointing out that Garr's strength had cleared half the first nest alone, that excluding him on principle rather than performance was its own kind of unfairness, that —

Hugo said nothing.

He had been standing slightly behind Seraph, arms at his sides, the particular stillness of a man managing something internal, something that pulled at him the way a current pulls at a swimmer who has decided, for now, to keep his feet on the riverbed. He absorbed the comment about Garr the way he absorbed most things directed at his brothers — with the quality of patience that was not quite patience, that was closer to a door held shut by force of will rather than ease, a door that strained faintly at its hinges with every word.

He absorbed the second comment the same way.

The third — the one about Seraph specifically, about what the Darkling were worth in the lower blocks, about the particular uses prisoners like him had once the regular work crews were done with them, said with a kind of casual cruelty that suggested the man had said it before, to other Darkling, in other corridors — that one Hugo did not absorb.

He moved forward.

Not a long movement. Not a dramatic one.

Just the deliberate closing of space that said certain things were finished.

The larger man read it correctly and stepped back, his hand drifting toward the club at his belt and then, seeing something in Hugo's face that made the calculation simple, stopping there.

His companion — the one with the wrapped arm, younger, the resemblance between them clearer up close, the set of the jaw, the particular angle of the brow — held still.

For a moment nothing happened.

Then the companion reached for the meat.

What followed was not a long fight. It was not a glorious one. It was three men — Hugo, then Garr when the second man entered it, moving with the same economical violence he'd used against the rats not twenty minutes before, no more emotion invested in one than the other — then the quiet competent efficiency of people who had survived this long by being exactly what was required and nothing more — and it ended with two men on the ground and nobody dead.

Daven watched from the wall. He did not intervene in either direction. His face, in the torchlight, held the particular blankness of a man filing the event away for later use, the way he seemed to file everything.

When it was over, Hugo stood breathing hard, hands open at his sides, knuckles already beginning to swell. He was not angry. That was the thing that distinguished it from rage — there was no heat in his face, no wildness, just the flat aftermath of necessity. Just the particular quiet of a man who had seen the line approach and stepped over it because there was no other direction available that didn't cost more than this one did.

The two men on the floor were not badly hurt. Hurt enough.

Garr gathered the meat without comment, dividing it with quick precise motions into six roughly equal portions, setting one aside for each man present including the two now sitting up against the wall, nursing bruises, watching him with expressions that had shifted somewhere in the last several minutes from contempt to something more careful.

Seraph said nothing for a long moment.

Then: "Thank you."

Hugo did not reply.

He didn't need to.

They left the two brothers where they were — upright now, holding their portions, saying nothing — and moved deeper into the corridor to finish the work detail. Nobody spoke about what had happened. There was nothing to say that the silence didn't already contain.

The corridor had a map.

Or it was supposed to.

The map was a smeared charcoal rendering on a square of cured leather, passed down through work details long enough that its original markings were barely legible beneath the amendments of everyone who had corrected it since — different hands, different pressures, lines crossed out and redrawn in a way that made the whole thing look less like a map and more like an argument that had been going on for years. It showed the primary corridors, the secondary junctions, the stairwells, each marked with small symbols whose meanings had mostly been lost along with whoever had first assigned them.

It did not show what was past the third junction.

Someone had written there, in different ink, at a different time, in a hand shakier than the rest: *past here nothing confirmed.*

Seraph studied this notation for a moment when they stopped to rest, tracing the words with one finger as if touch might extract more meaning from them than sight alone offered.

"What does that mean?" he asked.

Daven, who had remained with them after the brothers fell back to nurse their portion and their bruises in a side passage, glanced at it without much interest. "Means nobody came back to update the map," he said.

They moved on.

The third junction did not look different from the others. The corridor continued on the other side looking like every corridor — same stone, same moisture, same quality of dark, the torchlight throwing the same long shadows it had thrown for the last hour. The torch bent slightly toward a draft that had no obvious source, a thin cold current that none of them could trace to an opening, and that none of them remarked on, because in the Gate unexplained drafts were simply one more fact among many that didn't bear examining.

They took the left turn because the map suggested left, and because the smell of rat was stronger in that direction, and because the assignment was the assignment regardless of what the map didn't say.

The fourth junction had no marking at all.

Hugo stopped. Held the torch up, turning slowly, watching the light catch the stone from different angles.

The corridor ahead was older. Not slightly older — the distinction was visible in the stonework, the way the masonry shifted from the measured precision of intentional construction, blocks cut and fitted with the patience of skilled hands, to something rougher, less uniform, blocks of different sizes wedged together by builders who had cared more about speed than permanence. The walls here had been shaped rather than built. The ceiling was lower, forcing even Seraph to duck.

"We've come too far," Daven said quietly.

"We took the lefts," Seraph said. "The map said left."

"The map stopped saying anything three turns back."

They stood in the silence of that fact for a moment, the torch hissing softly, the only sound besides their own breathing.

Hugo turned around.

The corridor behind them looked the same as the corridor ahead — the same age, the same rough stone, the same low ceiling, as if they had passed through some unmarked threshold without noticing and had been walking in unfamiliar territory for longer than any of them had realized.

"Which way did we come from?" he said.

Nobody answered immediately.

They were not lost in the way of people who had made one wrong turn.

They were lost in the way of people who had made a series of small reasonable decisions inside a system that did not reward reason — a system older than anyone managing it, full of modifications and collapses and sealed sections and rerouted passages, a thing that had grown over generations until no single person understood the whole of it, not the overseer in his office three levels up, not the clerks who maintained the records, not the masons who occasionally came down to shore up a failing wall and left again as quickly as they could.

The Gate had not been designed to be knowable.

It had simply been added to.

They tried to retrace their steps and found that the corridor they had come through did not match their memory of it — not dramatically, not in any way that could be pointed to and named, just the particular wrongness of a space that had been modified enough times that it remembered several different versions of itself, layered one over another like the amendments on the leather map, until the original shape was as lost as everything else down here.

Daven sat down against a wall, sliding to the floor with the particular weariness of a man recalculating his odds.

"I've heard stories," he said. It was not a complete sentence. He didn't finish it, and for a moment the silence held the shape of whatever he hadn't said.

"About the lower sections," Seraph said.

Daven looked at him. "Old Cobb told you."

"Cobb told me something."

Daven was quiet for a moment, turning something over behind his eyes. "The guards sealed this level. Not recently — years ago. Before my time inside. Something happened with a work crew and they sealed it and filed it as a collapsed section." He paused. "I knew a clerk's runner, once, who saw the actual paperwork before it got buried in the archive. The crew was on the books as a transfer. That's what they do when they don't want to explain something. Transfer. Reassignment." He said the words with a particular flatness, the flatness of a man repeating language he had learned to distrust the exact moment he learned it. "Half the men who go missing down here are filed under one of those two words. Never dead. Never anything you could ask a follow-up question about."

The Gate's language. Everything had a proper word that meant something else.

"You knew," Hugo said. It was not an accusation.

"I suspected," Daven said. "I didn't know. There's a difference."

There was.

They rested briefly. Shared what water they had — not much, never much, doled out in careful mouthfuls that did less to satisfy thirst than to mark the passage of time. Then they moved, because resting in an unknown section of the Ninth Gate with a torch that would not last indefinitely was a form of dying by choice rather than circumstance, and none of them had survived this long by choosing the slower death over the faster uncertainty.

The forgotten sections were not dramatic.

That was the first thing Seraph noticed when they came to them — there was no threshold, no moment of crossing, no door to mark the boundary between the Gate that was used and the Gate that had been abandoned. The corridor simply became something different without announcing itself, the stonework growing older and then older still, degrading by degrees too gradual to mark, until there were things in the dark that did not belong to any active part of the institution.

A row of rusted shackles bolted to a wall, the bolt housing half-collapsed into stone, the iron eaten thin and lacy by decades of moisture, crumbling slightly when Hugo's torch passed close enough to warm it. Whatever the shackles had held was long gone.

A work station — a table, essentially, low and broad — with tools still laid out on its surface in a neat row, corroded past any use, arranged with the unmistakable care of someone who had expected to return to them. A chisel. A small hammer, its head rusted solid to the handle. A measuring cord, rotted to fragments, that had once perhaps been used to lay out the dimensions of whatever this place was meant to become.

A corridor branching left that ended after twenty paces in a collapse, rubble piled floor to ceiling, ancient and undisturbed. On the floor before the rubble, a boot. One boot. The leather had hardened to something like wood, curled at the toe, half buried in settled dust.

"Someone left in a hurry," Hugo said.

Or didn't leave, Seraph thought. He did not say it. Some thoughts, in the Gate, were better left exactly where they occurred to you.

The webbing appeared gradually, the way the older stonework had — first a thread here and there in the corners, easy to mistake for ordinary cobweb, the kind every cellar in Everterra collected. Then more. Then enough that they were moving through it rather than past it, tearing it aside with their hands, feeling it cling and stretch and finally part with a faint resistance that ordinary cobweb did not have. It was old web in places — dried and brittle, crumbling to powder at a touch — but layered over with newer threads that had the quality of something actively maintained, something tended, glistening faintly where the torchlight caught it at the right angle.

Garr stopped.

He crouched near the wall. Pressed two fingers into a thick cord of web and felt its tension, testing it the way he might test a rope before trusting his weight to it.

He looked back at Hugo.

Hugo looked at the web. At the corridor ahead, swallowed in shadow beyond the torch's reach. At the torch itself, which had burned down further than any of them liked to acknowledge.

"The stories were true," Seraph said quietly.

Not wonder. Not fear, exactly. The particular quality of a thing you had filed away as probably-not-real revealing itself as real, and all the space between those two states collapsing at once, leaving behind only the flat fact of it.

"We need to move," Hugo said.

They moved.

Daven stopped at the edge of where the webbing thickened, his torch held low, his face unreadable in the half-light.

"This is further than rat detail goes," he said. It was not quite a question.

"It is," Seraph said.

Daven looked past them, into the dark where the corridor curved out of sight, where the air had grown cold in a way that had nothing to do with the Gate's ordinary damp — a colder, stiller cold, the cold of a place that had not felt the warmth of a body passing through it in a very long time.

He did not say what he was thinking.

None of them needed him to.
