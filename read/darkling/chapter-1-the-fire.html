Darkling — Book I: Everterra

Chapter 1 — The Fire

The Ninth Gate was never meant to be lived in.

Seraph knew that before the guards ever touched him. He had known it for years, the way you know the shape of a scar without needing to look at it — by the way it pulled when he moved a certain direction, by the particular cold that lived in his chest whenever they took him toward the inner halls. The Gate taught you its truths slowly, then all at once.

"No."

The word was barely sound — more breath than defiance — but it was enough.

"Move."

The command came without heat or haste, spoken the way one spoke to doors or animals. Hands seized the back of Seraph's robe and wrenched him forward. He resisted instinctively, boots scraping against stone, fingers clawing for purchase as the open doorway yawned before him. The air beyond it felt wrong — colder, heavier, carrying a mineral wetness that had nothing to do with rain. It smelled the way old wells smelled. Stone that had never once been dry.

"Please," he said, voice thin, fraying. "Anywhere else. I'll clean anywhere else. The east latrines. The forge ash. Anywhere."

The blow came from the side. A fist drove into his ribs, sharp and precise, the kind of strike that came from a man who had done this so many times his body no longer needed instruction.

Pain flared — bright, familiar, survivable.

Seraph folded but did not let go. His fingers dug into the threshold, nails scraping stone raw, as if the doorway itself might offer mercy. He had learned, over years, which pains passed and which did not. Beatings passed. Interiors lingered.

"Inside," a guard growled. "You know the rules."

There were two of them on this rotation. Seraph knew their boots before he knew their faces — the worn one with the split sole that the taller guard never bothered repairing, and the newer pair on the shorter one, who'd transferred in from the southern garrison only a season ago and still spoke with that flatter accent. Neither of them hated him. That was the thing Seraph had never managed to explain to Hugo, not in any way that landed. They didn't hate him. Hatred required a kind of attention these two had stopped affording the inmates years before either of them had arrived. This was simply the shift. This was simply Tuesday, or whatever day it was — Seraph had lost the calendar somewhere in his second year and never found it again.

They peeled his hands loose one at a time.

The pain was manageable.

What waited beyond was not.

They threw him.

Seraph struck the stone on his hands and knees, the impact jarring his bones up through his wrists into his shoulders. Cold dampness soaked instantly into his palms and sleeves — he felt it bloom through the fabric, felt his skin react to it the way skin reacts to true cold, a tightening, an instinct to recoil from something the body understands as wrong before the mind catches up. Behind him, the guards laughed — not loudly, not cruelly, just mildly entertained, as men were when violence required no effort.

"Always this one," someone said. "Acts like the walls are going to eat him."

"Give it time," another replied. "They usually do."

The door slammed shut.

The sound of it did something to the room. It wasn't an echo exactly — more an absorption, the stone swallowing the noise of the slam and giving back only a faint resonance, like a struck bell wrapped in cloth. Seraph had noticed this before, in other chambers like this one. The deeper into the Gate you went, the less sound behaved the way sound was supposed to behave. As if the place had its own appetite for noise and metabolized it differently than ordinary stone.

Silence followed.

Not empty silence. Not peace.

A pressure. A presence.

Seraph remained where he had fallen, forehead hovering just above the stone, breath shallow and uneven. His ribs burned where they'd struck him — a dull, deep ache that promised to be worse tomorrow, the kind that settled into the muscle and stayed for days, making every breath a small negotiation. His hands trembled — not from pain, but from knowing where he was. The Ninth Gate did not merely contain rooms. It collected them. It remembered.

He had been beaten before.

Beatings passed.

The interior of the Ninth Gate did not.

He pressed his hands flat against the stone and breathed. One breath. Then another. The cold of the floor crept up through his palms into his wrists, into the bones of his forearms, patient and total, the way cold became in places that had never known a fire large enough to push it back permanently. He did not pray aloud — not here, not where the walls listened — but he held the habit of it in his chest like a coal held against cold. The Father was not a voice. Not a warmth, exactly. More the way a man holds certainty he cannot prove and carries it anyway, because the alternative is a different kind of weight entirely — the weight of believing that nothing watched, that nothing weighed any of it, that the years of this added up to precisely nothing.

He could not carry that weight. So he carried the other one.

The chamber around him was vast, its ceiling lost to shadow far above — vast in the particular way that frightened rather than comforted, because vastness in a place like this did not mean freedom, it meant more darkness to fill, more cold to circulate, more distance between himself and any door that might open. Water dripped steadily from unseen cracks, each drop echoing too long, as if the room were counting time on his behalf, marking some ledger he wasn't permitted to see. The air was cold and wet, heavy with mold, rot, and something older — something that had soaked into the stone over decades and refused to leave, a smell that lived beneath the smell of damp the way a memory lives beneath an emotion.

Then hands touched him.

Firm. Certain.

"Easy," a voice said close to his ear. Calm. Grounded. "I've got you."

Seraph didn't rise.

He was lifted.

Strong arms drew him upright before his legs could fail him, steadying him as the world stopped spinning, as the chamber's distant ceiling stopped its slow rotation and resolved back into stillness.

"You're shaking," the voice said.

"I'm fine," Seraph murmured automatically.

"You always say that," the voice replied.

When Seraph opened his eyes, there were three of them in the room.

Hugo stood closest — a broad, solid presence carved from endurance. Though shorter than most dwarves, his build carried unmistakable density: thick shoulders, powerful arms, weight that did not waste itself. His red hair hung long and bound into a single braid, his beard worn the same way, threaded with early gray that had arrived faster than it should have for a man his age — the Gate aged people unevenly, taking more from some than others, and it had taken its toll on Hugo through his hands first. They were scarred across the knuckles in a way that told you he'd been hitting things, or things had been hitting him, for a long time. His clothes were sackcloth and rags, patched in places with whatever scraps could be found, but he stood like a wall, positioned subtly between Seraph and the dark beyond the torchlight. There was something in the jaw, the particular set of it — something that did not belong to dwarf stock alone — but you would only notice if you were looking for it.

Behind them stood Garr.

Silent. Still.

The orc's presence was different — less weight, more balance. His frame was lean but powerful, muscle drawn tight beneath scarred skin, skin that told its own history in raised lines along his back and forearms, marks from beatings that had stopped, over the years, expecting any sound from him in return. His movements were economical, deliberate. Long black hair fell loose down his back, untouched by ornament. His eyes were calm, observant, unreadable. Dark eyes. The kind that held things without announcing them.

Seraph himself was pale even by Darkling standards — white skin stark against the gloom, black eyes reflecting torchlight like polished stone. Lavender horns curved smoothly from his brow, dulled by years of grime that no amount of washing in the cell troughs ever fully lifted. A tear in his robe revealed the slow movement of a double tail, twitching with nerves he could not still. He was thinner than he had been when they first found each other in this place — they all were — but Seraph's thinness had a different quality to it, the leanness of a body that gave more of itself to others than it kept.

Three figures.

Three breaths.

Together.

Seraph closed his eyes for a moment longer than necessary.

He had tried not to come this deep into the Gate. He always did. And the guards always made sure he paid for trying.

But he was not alone.

He never was.

The chamber they had been assigned was enormous, far larger than any task demanded of it. Once, it had likely hosted feasts or councils — stone laid to impress, to intimidate, to endure. You could see it in the bones of the place if you looked carefully: the wide arch of the doorway, the remnants of carved relief along one wall, worn nearly smooth by water and time, depicting something that might once have been a procession of figures and was now only suggestion. Now it stood hollow and stripped, its purpose reduced to neglect. The walls wept constantly, thin streams of water tracing paths into slick pools along the floor, pools that never fully drained, that had worn their own small basins into the stone over years of patient erosion. Debris lay scattered everywhere: broken furniture, shattered stone, rusted iron warped by age, the accumulated discard of however many decades the Gate had been using this room as a place to put things it no longer wanted to look at.

They began to clean.

Brooms scraped. Stone shifted. Old things were moved and burned. Work in the Ninth Gate was less about progress than obedience — proof that bodies could still be directed.

Seraph's hands had blistered, then calloused, over the years of this work. He remembered, distantly, a time when his hands had been soft — a memory so old now it felt like it belonged to someone else, a story he'd been told about a person he used to resemble.

"We've been here for years, Seraph," Hugo said, watching Garr move through controlled martial strikes against splintered furniture set upright like training foes. "And you still complain as if it will change our circumstances."

"I don't complain," Seraph said quietly. "I hope."

"That's what I said," Hugo replied.

Garr approached a solid oak table left inexplicably intact amid the general ruin — too heavy to have been carried out, too solid to have been worth the guards' effort to destroy. He placed his palm against it, drew a slow breath, and struck downward. The table split nearly in half with a crack that rolled briefly through the chamber and died. Garr centered himself again, hands pressed together, breath steady.

Both brothers were long accustomed to Garr's silence. He had taken a vow nearly ten years earlier — before the Gate, before any of this, a private decision made for private reasons that he had never explained and they had stopped asking him to. Not even relentless beatings from the guards had broken it. There had been a stretch, early in their time together, when certain guards had made a kind of game of trying — had beaten him specifically, deliberately, waiting for the sound that never came. Eventually they stopped. Not out of mercy. Out of boredom. A game only stayed interesting as long as there was a chance of winning it.

The scars along his back and calves told that story without words.

The work continued.

Stone shifted. Ash was gathered. Broken furniture was dragged closer to the fire pit and reduced piece by piece, the wood spitting and crackling, the smoke finding whatever crack in the ceiling led eventually to open air, somewhere far above them that none of the three had seen in longer than Seraph cared to count. The rhythm of labor settled back over them, familiar enough that Seraph almost forgot the tightness in his chest.

Almost.

They found the first nest late in the day — tucked beneath a collapsed section of wall, the stone hollowed by years of gnawing into something that looked almost deliberate, a kind of architecture built from patience and hunger. A dozen rats, maybe more, disturbed into fury by the work crew's approach. They were enormous. Each the size of a large dog, bodies swollen and corded with muscle, fur patchy and slick with filth, the kind of creature that should not have been able to survive on whatever scraps the Gate's lower levels provided and yet clearly had, generation after generation, growing larger and meaner with each one. Their eyes burned with fever-bright madness.

The three of them had been assigned rat work before.

They knew what to do.

Hugo moved first, drawing the creatures toward him with noise and motion, absorbing their attention, his boots planted wide and solid against the slick stone. Garr worked the edges — patient, precise, ending each rat cleanly before moving to the next, his strikes economical in a way that wasted nothing, not motion, not force, not time. Seraph stayed back, light gathering at his palms, using it not to fight but to drive, herding the ones that broke formation back toward the other two.

It wasn't elegant.

It was practiced.

The smell of it filled the chamber — blood and filth and the particular acridity of rat fear, a smell Seraph had stopped being able to separate from the Gate itself, so thoroughly had it woven into every memory of every chamber like this one.

When it was over, seven rats lay dead on the stone floor. Two had escaped into the walls. That was the way of it. You never killed them all. The Gate had been trying longer than any of them had been alive and the rats were still here.

Hugo crouched and began to dress the carcasses with the matter-of-fact efficiency of a man who had done this enough times that it required no thought — a quick economical motion with the small knife he was permitted to keep for exactly this purpose, the only blade any prisoner in this section was ever issued, tracked and counted at the start and end of every shift.

"Save the good cuts," Seraph said.

"I know," Hugo replied.

Garr was already building the fire low and narrow, coaxing the flame down until it barely breathed, feeding it the smaller, drier scraps first and watching the smoke carefully, adjusting the airflow with small movements of broken stone until it rose thin and pale rather than thick and visible. Anything that burned too brightly invited attention, and attention was never given without cost. Meat turned slowly on a rack of salvaged iron, fat hissing where it dripped into the coals.

There was a calculus to rat meat in the Ninth Gate, one that had taken Seraph the better part of a year to fully understand when he first arrived. The guards received food — not good food, not enough food, but food that arrived on a schedule and didn't have to be caught. Prisoners received what they could find, trade, or take. Rat meat sat near the top of what was findable. Salted and dried it lasted. It kept you working. It kept you breathing. It could be traded for thread, for a stolen blanket, for five minutes of a guard's deliberate inattention at the right moment. It was, by most measures, worth something — one of the only things in the Gate that a prisoner could call his own and mean it.

"Save some," Seraph said again, when the smell of cooking meat began to reach the corridor. "For the others."

Hugo looked at him. Not argument. Not quite agreement — the particular look of a man who has had this conversation many times before and has stopped expecting to win it, and has also, somewhere along the way, stopped entirely wanting to.

"There won't be much to save," he said.

"Whatever there is."

Hugo hesitated, then sighed. "You're impossible."

The others Seraph meant were the older prisoners in the adjacent cell block — those too weak or too broken for rat work, who survived on whatever was passed through bars by hands that had little enough to spare themselves. There was a man called Yorrin among them, missing most of one hand from an incident nobody spoke of directly, who had been inside longer than Seraph had been alive and who thanked him each time with the same three words, spoken so quietly they were almost only breath. There was a woman, Pell's age or older, whose name Seraph had never learned because she never offered it, who took what was given without comment and watched him with an expression he couldn't read.

Most of the Gate did not think about them. Most of the Gate could not afford to. Thinking about them required energy that hunger had already claimed.

Seraph thought about them anyway.

That was Seraph.

They ate what they needed. Wrapped what remained in cloth stripped from the hem of an already ruined garment, tied carefully so the smell wouldn't carry, so passing guards wouldn't think to ask questions whose answers would cost something.

When darkness fully settled — not true darkness, since the chamber's single torch never fully died, just the deeper version of dim that counted for night in a place that saw no sun — they lay close. Not for comfort, exactly. For certainty. For the simple animal reassurance of another body's warmth and breath nearby, proof against the particular loneliness the Gate specialized in manufacturing.

Morning would come whether they survived it or not.

Three bodies.

One warmth.

Held.

Rat work was routine in the Ninth Gate.

The assignments came down from the upper levels without explanation or sympathy — a corridor designation, a work order number, a tool allocation that never quite matched what the job required. Crews were assembled from whatever prisoners were available and sent in with brooms, clubs, and whatever courage they could scrape together.

Nobody expected the infestation to end.

That wasn't the point.

The point was that the rats needed to be pushed back periodically so the institution could continue functioning, and prisoners were the most economical way to push them. The crews came back thinner than they left, usually. Sometimes they came back with injuries. Occasionally they didn't come back at all, and a new work order was issued without comment, the missing names simply absent from the next roll call, nobody asking, nobody expected to ask.

In the cell blocks, the stories about missing crews circulated the way all stories circulated in the Gate — quietly, incompletely, amended by whoever was telling them until the original event was barely recognizable, the way water reshapes whatever it moves through.

An older man named Cobb, who had been inside longer than he could accurately count, told Seraph about the lower levels one evening while they shared a wall in the corridor outside the meal line, waiting for a ladle of grey stew that never quite filled the bowl it was given in. Cobb's face had the particular leather quality of skin that had spent decades indoors and underfed, lines etched deep enough to hold shadow even in torchlight.

"Before your time," Cobb said, without preamble, the way men spoke when they weren't sure they'd have another chance. "They used to send crews further down. Past the second delineation. There's whole sections the guards stopped going into."

"Why?" Seraph asked.

Cobb looked at him the way men looked when a question confirmed something they'd already suspected.

"Crews went in," he said. "Didn't come out."

He said it without drama. That was what made it land.

"They blamed the rats," he continued. "Made sense, mostly. Rats that size, enough of them — " He shrugged, a small economical motion, the shrug of a man who had used up his capacity for larger gestures somewhere in his first few years here. "But some of the guards said different things when they thought no one was listening. Said it wasn't rats. Said whatever's down there is something the rats avoid."

Seraph filed it away the way you filed things you couldn't afford to think about too much, the way he'd learned to file most things in the Gate — set aside, not forgotten, available for retrieval if it ever became useful or unavoidable.

A week later, Hugo heard a different version from a prisoner called Maret, who had worked maintenance before a broken arm — set badly, healed crooked — ended that assignment. Maret said the lower levels predated the current administration. Said there were records in the upper offices — he'd seen the covers once, being moved by a clerk in a hurry — that were older than anyone serving had been alive. Said the Gate had been built on top of something, and that whoever built it first hadn't finished what they started before they stopped building.

Hugo did not repeat this to Seraph.

He thought about it, alone, in the way he thought about things he couldn't do anything about — turning it over at night, setting it down, picking it back up days later when his mind had nothing better to occupy it.

Garr heard nothing specific. But he had spent enough time in the corridors to notice things the others didn't — that certain guard rotations never went past a particular point on the east passage, and that a sealed iron door near the lower stairwell had rust at its hinges that suggested it had not been opened in a generation, the kind of rust that came from decades of undisturbed moisture rather than the lighter surface rust of something handled regularly. He noticed the way some guards glanced at that door when they passed it — not fear, exactly. The particular blankness of people trained not to look at something, the studied incuriosity of men who had been told, at some point early in their service, that certain questions were not theirs to ask.

He said nothing.

He noted it.

That was Garr.
